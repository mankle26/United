Getting around around Munich

  • Mankle
  • Mar 23, 2023
  • 8 Comments

The public transport network of Munich (MVV) offers data on their services for free download here. Particularly interesting is their timetable data for bus stops around munich, which allows for network analysis. Usually applied to "social" networks in which the nodes (dots) are persons or accounts and their edges (connections) are some kind of interaction, public transfer systems can be studied through this approach as well. In this article we offer such an analysis, guided by the question:

How densely connected are the suburbs of Munich?

This Graph is available as svg-file for download here.

Getting on board

The data used for the above visualization is available in GTFS-Format (General Transit Feed Specification Reference). These are basically relational csv-files. Some pre-processing was necessary to transform these files to dyadic format, which is required for network analysis. In a dyadic data set, all entries/cases/rows are relations (edges) between two instances (nodes).

For this project, every relation is one entry of the timetable between two bus stops. If there were multiple connections a day, these were summed up. The same applies to different bus lines connecting the same stops. The sum of all these connections is the weight of the edge/connection. In the above graph this is represented as the width of the lines. The number of in- and outgoing buses at one stop is the degree of this node and represented as the size of the dot. Geodata for each stop allows a geographical layout.

First stop: Why does this matter?

The above map appears similar to the network maps provided by the MVV. However, in comparison to their visualizations, here the connections are as the crow flies. In addition, as just mentioned, the frequency of connections between two stops are printed as the width of the connection. A use case of such maps could be, if one wants to determine where to settle around Munich, based on how well-connected the town is within the public transport network.

Second stop: What is new?

We would like to highlight two interesting findings from this graphic:

The 'unclosed' circle

Although the towns around Munich are all connected among each other, the connection is more densely at some points than elsewhere. When zooming in, it becomes clear, that the circle is 'not closed' in the southwest of Munich (actually it is, but further from the center). Basically, getting from Unterhaching to Germering by MVV overland buses is much more difficult than getting from Germering to Dachau, although the direct distance is relatively similar.

The colors

Going back to the first graph, the network is colored in different colors. These do not represent real borders, zones or similar, but are rather the result of an algorithm highlighting stops with similar connections in the same color. Especially the red network with Fürstenfeldbruck in the center seems to be quite densely connected and span across a wide area.

Getting off: Isn´t there something missing?

Yes, there is obviously something missing in the white center of the graph: The city of Munich. Unfortunately, the available data only includes buses outside the city (MVV-Regionalbus). Therefore, the stops in the center could be displayes but would have no connections at all. This actually biases the network analyses, as for example Subway and "S-Bahn" are important not only for connecting the outskirts with the center but also the suburbs with each other. For the above example involving Unterhaching and Germering, this means that there are of course better ways to travel between these towns than going all the way to Wolfratshausen and then back north.

However, the analysis still offers some interesting insights and is also simply nice to look at. ;)
